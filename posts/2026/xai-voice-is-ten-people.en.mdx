The top 1% — five accounts — produced 35% of this cohort's posts over thirty days.

That number surprised me more than "52% never posted once." I already expected the silence. What I hadn't thought through was the other side of it — not that everyone else was talking in earnest, but that the voice was just as concentrated in a few hands.

## A cliff, not a slope

I laid out all 3,168 posts from those thirty days and sorted by author. The shape is a cliff, not a slope:

- Top 1% (five people): 35% of all posts
- Top 5% (24 people): 64%
- Top 10% (47 people): 77%

In an organization of 463 people, 244 (52%) never posted once. Most of those above that line surface a post now and then. The real voice sits with fewer than fifty people. And — here is the second layer of counterintuition — you think those fifty are "building a personal brand," "building in public"?

They're not.

## What the people who do talk are actually saying

Among the 70 most active accounts (≥10 posts in thirty days), I sorted by post type:

- Mostly **replies**: 31%
- Mostly **retweets**: 28%
- Mostly **original posts**: just 10%

Across the whole cohort, original posts are one in ten. The other nine are responding to someone, amplifying someone.

In other words: the "public voice of xAI" is an amplification layer, not a creation layer. Those five people, that dozen people, are mostly spreading, replying, taking a position with a retweet — not broadcasting original views of their own.

## Who are you tracking?

This reframed the structure of what "listening to xAI" even means.

When you say "what did xAI say on X lately," who exactly do you mean? Not the 463. It is the original posts from those five most active accounts, plus a layer of replies and retweets forming a voice on top. The 400 underneath consume quietly, drop a word under someone else's post now and then, and are basically invisible from the outside.

For a recruiter, an investor, or anyone trying to work out what this crowd is thinking, that is a very concrete problem:

Following 463 accounts down the timeline is not tracking 463 signals. It is 10 signals plus 453 pieces of noise dressed up as signal. Where you spend the effort completely changes what you end up finding.

## The barbed line

Giving an organization "a voice on X" never required everyone to talk — only that dozen amplifiers to keep amplifying, to keep retweeting at the right moment.

The problem: if you are an outsider trying to break into this circle, that amplification layer is opaque. You see the posts from those five people. What you don't see is who drives what they pay attention to, and whose silence is actually worth reading more than those five voices.

The signal is not on the timeline. It is in the structure — who is silent, who talks, who amplifies whom — and that structure has to be measured from the outside.
